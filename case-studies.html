Case 01 · Automation

Killing the meeting-notes tax

Junior Software Developer / Tech Lead · Ogilvy South Africa & Umuzi

Situation

After every Microsoft Teams meeting, someone had to write up notes and hand-create the follow-up tasks in Jira. It was the kind of small, repeated admin that quietly eats a team's week — easy to do badly, easy to forget, and nobody's favourite job.

What I did

I built an internal tool that captured the meeting notes automatically and synced the resulting action items straight into the right Jira boards. I focused on the handoff most likely to break — making sure a task landed where the owner would actually see it — rather than just dumping a transcript somewhere.

Outcome

The team stopped doing the write-up by hand. Action items stopped slipping through the gaps between a meeting ending and someone remembering to log them — the follow-through got more reliable, not just faster.

The win wasn't the automation. It was that people stopped dreading the ten minutes after every call.

Case 02 · Proactive support

Catching problems before users felt them

Junior Software Developer · Ogilvy South Africa

Situation

I supported production backend services that real teams depended on daily. When something broke, the first anyone usually knew was a user reporting it — which meant the problem had already reached the person it was going to inconvenience.

What I did

I added logging, monitoring, and performance instrumentation across the services so issues surfaced as signals we could see, not as complaints we had to wait for. When something did go wrong, I worked the incident to root cause and wrote up troubleshooting guides so the next person — including teammates on other shifts — could resolve it without starting from scratch.

Outcome

User-reported incidents dropped, because more were caught and fixed before users hit them. The documentation meant a problem solved once stayed solved, instead of being rediscovered by whoever was on next.

Case 03 · Build under pressure

Safe Spaces, built in 48 hours

FNB App of the Year Hackathon · MVP

Situation

A 48-hour hackathon brief: build something genuinely useful, end to end, with a real user in mind — and have it working, not just designed, by the deadline.

What I did

My team built Safe Spaces, a real-time safety-mapping web app. I designed the backend APIs handling geolocation data, user submissions, and reporting, and built modular front-end components so we could move fast without the UI falling apart under change. The constraint that mattered most was time, so I prioritised the few features a user would actually touch and cut everything that wouldn't show in a demo.

Outcome

We shipped a working MVP inside the window — a live, usable product rather than a slide deck. It's the clearest evidence I can offer of working fast and calm when the clock is the hardest part of the problem.

Case 04 · Enablement

Turning stuck developers into shipping ones

Junior Tech Lead · Umuzi.org

Situation

I led a squad of junior developers. The recurring challenge wasn't the code — it was the moments someone hit a wall, lost confidence, and went quiet instead of asking. Left alone, those moments stall a whole delivery.

What I did

I ran the standups, sprint planning and code reviews, but the part that mattered was spotting the stall early — noticing who'd gone quiet — and pairing with them to get unblocked before frustration set in. I gave feedback directly and kindly, and used the reviews to teach, not just to gatekeep quality.

Outcome

Quality and delivery held across concurrent projects, and developers got unstuck faster. The same instinct sits behind good customer success: catch the frustrated moment early, and engineer more of the confident ones.
